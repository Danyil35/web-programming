Personal computers, which emerged in the 1980s, truly represented a revolutionary step in the history of computing technology. They brought computing into homes and offices, changing the way people work, learn, and relax.

IBM PC - 1981

The IBM PC, introduced on August 12, 1981, is considered one of the most significant milestones in the history of personal computers. Its official name was IBM 5150, and this computer became the foundation for the PC standard that was actively used and developed in the following decades. The IBM PC established standards that became the basis for subsequent models, including compatibility with hardware and software solutions. This facilitated the creation of a vast array of software and accessories for PCs, as well as the emergence of numerous clones and compatible computers from other manufacturers.

Apple Macintosh - 1984

The Apple Macintosh was announced on January 24, 1984, and became the first personal computer to offer a graphical user interface (GUI) and a mouse as standard input devices. This computer showcased a new approach to interacting with computers, significantly simplifying their use. The Macintosh was revolutionary due to its graphical interface, which included windows, icons, and menus, making computers more accessible to non-professional users. This contributed to the popularization of GUIs and later developments in the field of personal computers.

Commodore 64 - 1982

The Commodore 64, introduced in August 1982, was one of the most popular home computers of the 1980s, known for its affordable price and wide range of software. The Commodore 64 became popular among home users due to its low cost and rich selection of games and applications. With a vast amount of available software and support for various media formats (cartridge games, cassettes, and later floppy disks), it became one of the best-selling home computers of all time.

The personal computers of the 1980s were not just technological innovations; they also changed the social structure, making computing technology accessible to the general public. The IBM PC laid the groundwork for modern PC systems, the Macintosh introduced a graphical interface that simplified computer usage, and the Commodore 64 brought computing capabilities into the homes of millions. These innovations formed the foundation for the further development of computing technology and information technology.
